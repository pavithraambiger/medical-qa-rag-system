DIABETES
Diabetes is a chronic disease that occurs when the pancreas does not produce enough insulin or when the body cannot effectively use the insulin it produces.
Symptoms of diabetes include frequent urination, excessive thirst, unexplained weight loss, extreme fatigue, blurry vision, slow-healing cuts or bruises, and tingling or numbness in hands or feet.
Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition where the immune system attacks insulin-producing cells. It requires daily insulin injections.
Type 2 diabetes is the most common form. The body either resists insulin or doesn't produce enough. It is managed through diet, exercise, and medication.
Risk factors for diabetes include obesity, physical inactivity, family history, age over 45, high blood pressure, and high cholesterol.
Complications of diabetes include heart disease, kidney damage (nephropathy), eye damage (retinopathy), nerve damage (neuropathy), and foot problems.
Diagnosis is done through fasting blood glucose test, HbA1c test, or oral glucose tolerance test.

HYPERTENSION (HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE)
Hypertension is a condition where blood pressure in the arteries is persistently elevated, typically above 130/80 mmHg.
Most people with hypertension have no symptoms, which is why it is called the silent killer.
Risk factors include obesity, high salt diet, smoking, excessive alcohol, stress, family history, and age.
Treatment includes lifestyle changes such as reducing salt intake, regular exercise, weight loss, limiting alcohol, and quitting smoking.
Medications for hypertension include ACE inhibitors, calcium channel blockers, beta blockers, and diuretics.
Uncontrolled hypertension can lead to heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, and vision loss.

CHRONIC KIDNEY DISEASE (CKD)
Chronic kidney disease is the gradual loss of kidney function over time.
The kidneys filter waste and excess fluids from the blood. When damaged, waste builds up in the body.
Main causes of CKD are diabetes and high blood pressure, which together account for most cases.
Symptoms include fatigue, swollen ankles, shortness of breath, blood in urine, foamy urine, and difficulty sleeping.
CKD is diagnosed through blood tests (creatinine, GFR) and urine tests (albumin).
Stages of CKD range from Stage 1 (mild) to Stage 5 (kidney failure requiring dialysis or transplant).
Treatment includes controlling blood sugar, managing blood pressure, low-protein diet, and avoiding NSAIDs.

BRAIN STROKE
A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is cut off or a blood vessel bursts.
Types include ischemic stroke (blockage) which accounts for 87% of strokes, and hemorrhagic stroke (bleeding).
Risk factors for brain stroke include high blood pressure, diabetes, smoking, obesity, heart disease, high cholesterol, and age above 55.
Symptoms of stroke follow the FAST acronym: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency.
Other symptoms include sudden severe headache, confusion, vision problems, and loss of balance.
Treatment for ischemic stroke includes clot-busting drugs (tPA) given within 4.5 hours of onset.
Prevention includes controlling blood pressure, quitting smoking, regular exercise, healthy diet, and managing diabetes.

LIVER DISEASE
The liver performs over 500 functions including detoxification, protein synthesis, and digestion support.
Common liver diseases include fatty liver disease, hepatitis, cirrhosis, and liver cancer.
Fatty liver disease is the accumulation of fat in liver cells, often linked to obesity, diabetes, and alcohol use.
Hepatitis is inflammation of the liver caused by viral infection (Hepatitis A, B, C), alcohol, or medications.
Cirrhosis is scarring of liver tissue caused by long-term liver damage, often from alcohol or hepatitis.
Symptoms of liver disease include jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes), abdominal pain, swelling, fatigue, nausea, and dark urine.
Diagnosis is done through liver function tests (LFTs), ultrasound, CT scan, and liver biopsy.
Treatment depends on the cause — antiviral medications for hepatitis, lifestyle changes for fatty liver, and liver transplant for end-stage disease.

LUNG CANCER
Lung cancer is one of the most common and serious cancers worldwide.
Main types are non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) which accounts for 85% of cases, and small cell lung cancer (SCLC).
Risk factors include smoking (responsible for 85% of cases), secondhand smoke, radon gas exposure, asbestos, air pollution, and family history.
Symptoms include persistent cough, coughing up blood, chest pain, shortness of breath, hoarseness, weight loss, and fatigue.
Diagnosis involves chest X-ray, CT scan, PET scan, bronchoscopy, and biopsy.
Treatment options include surgery, radiation therapy, chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy depending on the stage.
Early detection significantly improves survival rates. Low-dose CT screening is recommended for high-risk individuals.

HEART DISEASE
Heart disease refers to several types of heart conditions including coronary artery disease, heart failure, and arrhythmias.
Coronary artery disease is caused by plaque buildup in arteries, reducing blood flow to the heart.
Symptoms include chest pain (angina), shortness of breath, fatigue, and swelling in legs.
Risk factors include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, diabetes, obesity, and family history.
Heart attack occurs when blood flow to part of the heart is blocked. Symptoms include chest pain, arm pain, sweating, and nausea.
Treatment includes medications (statins, aspirin, beta blockers), lifestyle changes, angioplasty, and bypass surgery.
Prevention includes regular exercise, healthy diet low in saturated fats, not smoking, and controlling blood pressure and cholesterol.

MENTAL HEALTH
Mental health includes emotional, psychological, and social well-being affecting how we think, feel, and act.
Common mental health disorders include depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, and PTSD.
Depression symptoms include persistent sadness, loss of interest, sleep problems, fatigue, and difficulty concentrating.
Anxiety disorders involve excessive fear or worry that interferes with daily activities.
Treatment for mental health conditions includes psychotherapy, medications (antidepressants, anti-anxiety drugs), and lifestyle changes.
Risk factors include genetic predisposition, trauma, chronic illness, substance abuse, and social isolation.
Early intervention and seeking professional help are key to managing mental health conditions effectively.
